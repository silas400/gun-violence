Gun Violence Facts

390,000 students faced school gun violence since 1999.

Since the Columbine shooting in 1999, more than 390,000 U.S. students have been exposed to gun violence at school. In 2022 alone, over 43,000 children faced gunfire on school grounds, showing both the long-term impact and the alarming scale of recent years.

Sandy Hook Promise

2021 had the most school shootings recorded.

The 2021–22 school year had more shootings than any other since tracking began in 2000. This shows school violence reached its highest level in over 20 years. The increase highlights the growing danger students face on campus. It also points to the need for stronger prevention and safety measures.

National Center for Education Statistics

The U.S. has far more school shootings than other nations.

Research shows the U.S. has had 57 times as many school shootings as all other major industrialized countries combined. Guns are also the leading cause of death for American children and teens, with youth being many times more likely to be killed by firearms compared to peers in other wealthy nations. These risks disproportionately harm Black and Latinx children, and exposure to such violence often leads to depression, PTSD, substance abuse, and school failure.

National Library of Medicine (PMC)

Nearly all mass shooters are male.

From 1966 to 2021, research shows that 98% of mass shooting perpetrators identified as male. This overwhelming gender imbalance highlights how masculinity, social pressures, and access to firearms intersect in these violent acts. Understanding the role of gender is key to developing more effective prevention strategies and addressing the cultural factors that contribute to mass violence.

The Violence Project

Warning Signs & Firearm Access

Most school shooters showed struggles before attacking.

Research shows that many school shooters faced serious stressors like abuse, academic pressure, or failed relationships, and most displayed signs of mental health concerns. These struggles often appeared well before violence occurred, highlighting the need for early support and prevention.

Sandy Hook Promise

Most under-18 shooters get guns from their home.

About 4.6 million U.S. children live in homes with at least one loaded, unlocked gun. Since the vast majority of school shooters under 18 access firearms from their household, secure storage and child-access prevention (CAP) laws are critical. Safe storage could stop many tragedies before they begin.

Everytown Research

The Impact on Survivors

School shootings harm education and attendance.

Survivors are more likely to miss school, repeat grades, and see test scores drop. Research shows enrollment falls and absenteeism rises significantly in the two years following a shooting, disrupting both students’ progress and school stability.

Stanford SIEPR

Survivors face long-term setbacks in life.

Students exposed to shootings are less likely to graduate high school, attend college, or complete degrees. By their mid-20s, they have lower employment rates and earn about $115,550 less over a lifetime compared to peers.

Stanford SIEPR

Mental health worsens after exposure.

Antidepressant prescriptions rise sharply for students near shooting sites, showing long-term trauma effects. Survivors also face higher risks of suicide and accidental deaths, highlighting the lasting psychological toll.

Stanford SIEPR

Prevention Strategies

Positive school climate reduces violence.

Schools that actively prevent bullying, harassment, and discrimination create safer and more welcoming environments. When students feel respected and supported by peers and staff, they are less likely to engage in aggression. This sense of safety not only reduces violence but also boosts attendance, motivation, and academic outcomes.

University of Virginia

Storage laws can be effective.

Nearly three-quarters of school shooters under 18 got their guns from home or a relative’s home. With over 4 million children living in houses where firearms are left loaded and unlocked, the danger is real. Secure storage and CAP laws stop kids from getting easy access to guns, making them one of the most effective ways to prevent school shootings before they happen.

Every Town

Threat assessment teams could reduce school shootings.

Unlike lockdown drills, which only prepare students to survive an attack, threat assessment teams proactively identify and respond to warning signs. These multidisciplinary groups help distinguish real threats from false alarms and support students in crisis, lowering suspensions, expulsions, and arrests. By intervening early, threat assessment could potentially stop a tragedy before it becomes a mass shooting.

WestEd

Mental health staffing prevents violence.

Adequate counselors, psychologists, and social workers play a vital role in identifying and supporting struggling students before problems escalate into crises. Mental health staff provide early intervention, coping strategies, and safe outlets for stress. Research shows that access to these services reduces risks of violence and improves long-term student well-being.

University of Virginia

Discipline reform and coordination save lives.

Harsh suspensions and zero-tolerance policies often push students further toward disengagement and anger, while supportive discipline helps redirect behavior in healthier ways. At the same time, effective communication across schools, law enforcement, and mental health professionals allows early warning signs to be acted on before tragedy occurs. Together, these practices create safer school systems and stronger community trust.

University of Virginia

Systemic Failures & Broader Impacts

Lawmakers repeatedly ignore calls for action after shootings.

After nearly every mass shooting, survivors and communities call for stronger gun laws, yet political leaders often fail to act. This cycle of outrage followed by inaction leaves the root causes of violence unaddressed and contributes to repeated tragedies over time.

PBS NewsHour

Educators also suffer trauma from school gun violence.

Gun violence impacts not just students but also the teachers and staff who guide them daily. Educators often experience overlooked stress, fear, and long-term trauma, which can erode their well-being and drive them from the profession. Expanding mental health resources for teachers is essential to sustain a strong, stable workforce in schools.

PBS NewsHour

Myths & Misconceptions

Armed officers don’t reduce casualties.

A study of 133 school shootings from 1980–2019 found that schools with an armed officer on scene had nearly three times more deaths compared to those without one. The presence of guards showed no reduction in injuries, and researchers noted that many shooters, often suicidal, may even be encouraged rather than deterred by armed resistance. Prevention and early intervention efforts are more effective than relying on armed guards.

JAMA Network Open

There is no single profile of a school shooter.

Research shows school shooters come from diverse demographics, family backgrounds, and academic levels. Some are socially isolated while others are well connected, making prediction by profile unreliable. This underscores the importance of focusing on behaviors and warning signs rather than stereotypes.

WestEd

School Resource Officers don’t reduce shootings.

School Resource Officers (SROs) are sworn law enforcement officers placed in schools to provide security and build relationships with students. Research shows SROs fail to lower deaths or injuries in shootings. Instead, their presence increases suspensions, expulsions, arrests, absenteeism, and even lowers graduation rates. These harms fall hardest on Black, Indigenous, Latinx students, and students with disabilities, underscoring the need for strict limits on SRO roles and mandatory training.
